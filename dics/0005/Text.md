# Working from home
Working from home has become a significant feature of the modern labour market.
During the COVID-19 pandemic, remote work was rapidly adopted by organisations across the world.
According to recent studies, more than 40 percent of employees worked remotely in 2020.
By 2023, this number had decreased to approximately 28 percent, although hybrid models remain widely used.
Furthermore, it has been observed that remote work allows employees to save both time and money on commuting.
On average, workers may save between 5 and 10 hours per week, which can be reinvested into personal or professional activities.
In addition, greater flexibility is often provided, which enables individuals to manage their schedules more effectively.
As a result, higher levels of job satisfaction have been reported in several surveys.
However, certain challenges must also be considered.
Communication may be weakened if teams are not properly managed.
If regular interaction is not maintained, collaboration can become less effective.
Moreover, productivity may be affected in situations where clear structures are not established.
It has been suggested that employees perform better when expectations are clearly defined.
Nevertheless, some organisations have required staff to return to the office.
This decision is often justified by the need to improve team cohesion and long term performance.
If hybrid systems are implemented effectively, a balance between flexibility and collaboration can be achieved.
Consequently, many experts believe that a mixed model will dominate the future of work.
Thus, working from home should not be seen as a temporary solution, but as a long term transformation that requires careful planning and adaptation.
